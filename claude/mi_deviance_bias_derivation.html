1. Proof Sketch

We derive the bias that arises when estimating the complete-data log-likelihood using imputation. The key result is that this bias is unavoidable when the imputation parameter is estimated from data. While the bias can in principle be corrected by estimating $\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)$, existing methods such as AICcd do not include this correction.

Step 1: Setup and Definitions

Let $Y = (Y^{obs}, Y^{mis})$ denote the complete data. The complete-data log-likelihood decomposes as:

$$\ell_{com}(\theta) = \ell_{obs}(\theta) + \ell_{mis|obs}(\theta)$$

The Q-function is defined as:

$$Q(\theta|\tilde{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{com}(\theta)]$$
Step 2: Decomposing the Q-function

Since $\ell_{obs}(\theta)$ does not depend on $Y^{mis}$:

$$Q(\theta|\tilde{\theta}) = \ell_{obs}(\theta) + \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta)]$$
Step 3: The Bias We Want to Compute
$$\text{Bias} = \mathbb{E}[Q(\hat{\theta}|\tilde{\theta})] - \mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})]$$
Key observation: If $\tilde{\theta} = \theta^*$ (the true parameter), then $\text{Bias} = 0$ by the law of iterated expectations. The bias arises precisely because $\tilde{\theta}$ is estimated from data.
Step 4: Taylor Expansion of $\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})$
$$\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}) = \ell_{com}(\theta^*) + (\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T S_{com}(\theta^*) - \frac{1}{2}(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathcal{J}_{com}(\theta^*)(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*) + R_3$$

where $S_{com}(\theta^*) = \partial_\theta \ell_{com}(\theta^*)$ is the score, $\mathcal{J}_{com}(\theta^*) = -\partial_\theta^2 \ell_{com}(\theta^*)$ is the observed information, and $R_3 = O_p(N^{-3/2})$.

Step 5: Taylor Expansion of $Q(\hat{\theta}|\tilde{\theta})$
$$Q(\hat{\theta}|\tilde{\theta}) = Q(\theta^*|\tilde{\theta}) + (\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{com}(\theta^*)]$$ $$- \frac{1}{2}(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\mathcal{J}_{com}(\theta^*)](\hat{\theta}-\theta^*) + R_3$$
Step 6: Taking the Difference
$$Q(\hat{\theta}|\tilde{\theta}) - \ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}) = \underbrace{\left[Q(\theta^*|\tilde{\theta}) - \ell_{com}(\theta^*)\right]}_{\text{Term 1}}$$ $$+ \underbrace{(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{com}(\theta^*)] - S_{com}(\theta^*)\right]}_{\text{Term 2}}$$ $$- \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\mathcal{J}_{com}(\theta^*)] - \mathcal{J}_{com}(\theta^*)\right](\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)}_{\text{Term 3}} + O_p(N^{-3/2})$$
Step 7: Applying the Missing Information Principle

Using the decompositions:

  • $S_{com} = S_{obs} + S_{mis|obs}$
  • $\mathcal{J}_{com} = \mathcal{J}_{obs} + \mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}$

The "obs" components pass through the conditional expectation $\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\cdot]$ unchanged and cancel in the differences, leaving only the "mis|obs" components.

Step 8: Substituting into the Difference

All three terms now involve only the missing-data components:

$$Q(\hat{\theta}|\tilde{\theta}) - \ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}) = \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \ell_{mis|obs,\theta^*}(\theta^*)\right]$$ $$+ (\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - S_{mis|obs,\theta^*}(\theta^*)\right]$$ $$- \frac{1}{2}(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \mathcal{J}_{mis|obs,\theta^*}(\theta^*)\right](\hat{\theta}-\theta^*) + O_p(N^{-3/2})$$

where $\ell_{mis|obs,\theta^*}$ denotes $\ell_{mis|obs}$ evaluated at the true $Y^{mis}$ (drawn from $p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*)$).

Step 9: Taking the Full Expectation
$$\text{Bias} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \ell_{mis|obs,\theta^*}(\theta^*)\right]}_{\text{Term 1}}$$ $$+ \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - S_{mis|obs,\theta^*}(\theta^*)\right]\right]}_{\text{Term 2}}$$ $$- \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \mathcal{J}_{mis|obs,\theta^*}(\theta^*)\right](\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)\right]}_{\text{Term 3}} + O(N^{-3/2})$$
Step 10: Analyzing Term 1

Term 1 becomes:

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{obs}}\left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]\right]$$

This is the difference in expected log-likelihood when imputing under $\tilde{\theta}$ versus the true $\theta^*$.

Step 11: Evaluating the Difference in Conditional Expectations

Taylor expanding $p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta})$ around $\tilde{\theta} = \theta^*$ and using $\partial_{\tilde{\theta}}p = p \cdot S_{mis|obs}$:

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]$$ $$\approx (\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]$$
Step 12: Term 1 is Zero (to first order)

By integration by parts (or the score identity):

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] = -\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] = 0$$

So Term 1 contributes only $O(N^{-1})$ from higher-order terms.

Step 13: Analyzing Term 2

We need:

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]$$

Since $\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] = 0$, Taylor expanding gives:

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] \approx \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)^T](\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)$$ $$= \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)$$
Step 14: Substituting Back into Term 2

Term 2 becomes:

$$\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)\right]$$

The $S_{mis|obs,\theta^*}(\theta^*)$ component vanishes because $\mathbb{E}[S_{mis|obs,\theta^*}(\theta^*)|Y^{obs}] = 0$.

Step 15: Term 3 is Higher Order

Term 3 involves:

  • $(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T[\cdot](\hat{\theta}-\theta^*) = O_p(N^{-1})$
  • multiplied by a difference that is $O(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*) = O_p(N^{-1/2})$

Therefore Term 3 contributes $O(N^{-3/2})$.

Leading-order bias: $$\text{Bias} = \mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)\right] + O(N^{-3/2})$$
Step 16: Converting to Trace Form

Using the identity $a^T B c = \text{tr}(B c a^T)$ and taking expectations:

$$\text{Bias} = \text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \mathbb{E}[(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T]\right) + O(N^{-3/2})$$

Assuming $\tilde{\theta}$ and $\hat{\theta}$ are both asymptotically unbiased for $\theta^*$:

Final Result: $$\boxed{\text{Bias} = \text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \text{Cov}(\tilde{\theta}, \hat{\theta})\right) + O(N^{-3/2})}$$

Special Case: MLE Imputation ($\tilde{\theta} = \hat{\theta}$)

When imputation uses the same MLE that is being evaluated:

$$\text{Bias} = \text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})\right) + O(N^{-3/2})$$
Important: Here $\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})$ is the variance of the MLE from the observed-data likelihood: $$\hat{\theta}_{obs} = \arg\max_\theta \ell_{obs}(\theta | Y^{obs})$$ Under standard regularity conditions: $$\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}) \approx \mathcal{I}_{obs}(\theta^*)^{-1}/N$$ This is not the variance we would have from maximizing the complete-data likelihood.

Substituting:

MLE Imputation Bias: $$\text{Bias} = \frac{1}{N}\text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}(\theta^*)^{-1}\right) + O(N^{-3/2})$$

Why the Bias is Positive

  • $\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)$ is positive semi-definite (it's a Fisher information matrix)
  • $\text{Cov}(\tilde{\theta}, \hat{\theta})$ is positive semi-definite (it's a covariance matrix)
  • For positive semi-definite matrices $A$ and $B$: $\text{tr}(AB) \geq 0$
  • Strictly positive when parameters have both nonzero uncertainty and information from missing data

Why Correction is Impossible

The correction formula requires $\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)$—the missing-data information matrix evaluated at the true parameter.

This quantity is fundamentally unobservable:

  • $\theta^*$ is unknown (that's why we're estimating it)
  • $\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}$ characterizes information from unobserved data
  • Estimating it from imputed data creates circular dependence on $\tilde{\theta}$

This is why complete-data model selection cannot be replicated under missing data—the bias correction requires quantities that cannot be known.

2. Detailed Derivation

We now provide the complete derivation with all intermediate calculations.

Step 1: Setup and Definitions

Let $Y = (Y^{obs}, Y^{mis})$ denote the complete data, where $Y^{obs}$ is observed and $Y^{mis}$ is missing. The complete-data log-likelihood can be decomposed as:

$$\ell_{com}(\theta) = \ell_{obs}(\theta) + \ell_{mis|obs}(\theta)$$

where:

  • $\ell_{obs}(\theta) = \log p(Y^{obs}|\theta)$ is the observed-data log-likelihood
  • $\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta) = \log p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta)$ is the conditional log-likelihood of the missing data given the observed data

This decomposition follows from the factorization $p(Y|\theta) = p(Y^{obs}|\theta) \cdot p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta)$.

Define the Q-function as the expected complete-data log-likelihood, where the expectation is over the conditional distribution of $Y^{mis}$ given $Y^{obs}$ and a conditioning parameter $\tilde{\theta}$:

$$Q(\theta|\tilde{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{com}(\theta)]$$

We also define:

  • $\theta^*$ = the true (population) parameter value
  • $\hat{\theta} = \arg\max_\theta \ell_{obs}(\theta|Y^{obs})$ = the MLE from the observed-data likelihood
  • $\tilde{\theta}$ = the parameter used for imputation (may equal $\hat{\theta}$ or be a draw from a posterior)

Step 2: Decomposing the Q-function

Substituting the decomposition of $\ell_{com}$ into the Q-function:

$$Q(\theta|\tilde{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{obs}(\theta) + \ell_{mis|obs}(\theta)]$$

Since $\ell_{obs}(\theta) = \log p(Y^{obs}|\theta)$ depends only on $Y^{obs}$ and not on $Y^{mis}$, it passes through the conditional expectation:

$$Q(\theta|\tilde{\theta}) = \ell_{obs}(\theta) + \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta)]$$

This decomposition is crucial: the observed-data component is unchanged, while only the missing-data component involves the imputation distribution.

Step 3: The Bias We Want to Compute

The bias in using the Q-function to estimate the complete-data log-likelihood is:

$$\text{Bias} = \mathbb{E}[Q(\hat{\theta}|\tilde{\theta})] - \mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})]$$

where the outer expectation is over the joint distribution of $(Y^{obs}, Y^{mis})$ from the true data-generating process $p(Y|\theta^*)$.

Key observation: If $\tilde{\theta} = \theta^*$ (the true parameter), then by the law of iterated expectations: $$\mathbb{E}[Q(\hat{\theta}|\theta^*)] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})]] = \mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})]$$ so the bias would be exactly zero. The bias arises precisely because $\tilde{\theta}$ is estimated from data and therefore $\tilde{\theta} \neq \theta^*$.

Step 4: Taylor Expansion of $\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})$

We expand $\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})$ around the true parameter $\theta^*$ using a second-order Taylor expansion:

$$\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}) = \ell_{com}(\theta^*) + (\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \partial_\theta \ell_{com}(\theta^*) + \frac{1}{2}(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \partial_\theta^2 \ell_{com}(\theta^*)(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*) + R_3$$

Define the score and observed information:

  • Score: $S_{com}(\theta) = \partial_\theta \ell_{com}(\theta)$
  • Observed information: $\mathcal{J}_{com}(\theta) = -\partial_\theta^2 \ell_{com}(\theta)$

Then:

$$\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}) = \ell_{com}(\theta^*) + (\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T S_{com}(\theta^*) - \frac{1}{2}(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathcal{J}_{com}(\theta^*)(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*) + R_3$$

The remainder term $R_3$ involves third derivatives:

$$R_3 = \frac{1}{6}\sum_{j,k,l} \frac{\partial^3 \ell_{com}}{\partial\theta_j\partial\theta_k\partial\theta_l}\bigg|_{\bar{\theta}} (\hat{\theta}_j-\theta^*_j)(\hat{\theta}_k-\theta^*_k)(\hat{\theta}_l-\theta^*_l)$$

Since $\|\hat{\theta}-\theta^*\| = O_p(N^{-1/2})$, we have $R_3 = O_p(N^{-3/2})$.

Step 5: Taylor Expansion of $Q(\hat{\theta}|\tilde{\theta})$

Similarly, we expand $Q(\hat{\theta}|\tilde{\theta})$ around $\theta^*$. Since $Q(\theta|\tilde{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{com}(\theta)]$, we have:

$$\partial_\theta Q(\theta|\tilde{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\partial_\theta \ell_{com}(\theta)] = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{com}(\theta)]$$
$$\partial_\theta^2 Q(\theta|\tilde{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\partial_\theta^2 \ell_{com}(\theta)] = -\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\mathcal{J}_{com}(\theta)]$$

Therefore:

$$Q(\hat{\theta}|\tilde{\theta}) = Q(\theta^*|\tilde{\theta}) + (\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{com}(\theta^*)]$$ $$- \frac{1}{2}(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\mathcal{J}_{com}(\theta^*)](\hat{\theta}-\theta^*) + R_3$$

Step 6: Taking the Difference

Subtracting the expansion from Step 4 from the expansion in Step 5:

$$Q(\hat{\theta}|\tilde{\theta}) - \ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}) = \underbrace{\left[Q(\theta^*|\tilde{\theta}) - \ell_{com}(\theta^*)\right]}_{\text{Term 1: Zeroth-order}}$$
$$+ \underbrace{(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{com}(\theta^*)] - S_{com}(\theta^*)\right]}_{\text{Term 2: First-order}}$$
$$- \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\mathcal{J}_{com}(\theta^*)] - \mathcal{J}_{com}(\theta^*)\right](\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)}_{\text{Term 3: Second-order}} + O_p(N^{-3/2})$$

Step 7: Applying the Missing Information Principle

The score and observed information decompose according to the missing information principle:

$$S_{com}(\theta) = S_{obs}(\theta) + S_{mis|obs}(\theta)$$
$$\mathcal{J}_{com}(\theta) = \mathcal{J}_{obs}(\theta) + \mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta)$$

where:

  • $S_{obs}(\theta) = \partial_\theta \ell_{obs}(\theta)$ depends only on $Y^{obs}$
  • $S_{mis|obs}(\theta) = \partial_\theta \ell_{mis|obs}(\theta)$ depends on both $Y^{obs}$ and $Y^{mis}$

Since $S_{obs}(\theta)$ and $\mathcal{J}_{obs}(\theta)$ depend only on $Y^{obs}$, they pass through the conditional expectation unchanged:

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{obs}(\theta^*)] = S_{obs}(\theta^*)$$
$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\mathcal{J}_{obs}(\theta^*)] = \mathcal{J}_{obs}(\theta^*)$$

Therefore, in the differences appearing in Terms 1, 2, and 3, the "obs" components cancel.

Step 8: Substituting into the Difference

After cancellation of the "obs" components, all three terms involve only the "mis|obs" components:

Term 1:

$$Q(\theta^*|\tilde{\theta}) - \ell_{com}(\theta^*) = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)$$

Here $\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)$ on the right-hand side is evaluated at the actual $Y^{mis}$ (drawn from the true distribution $p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*)$).

Term 2:

$$(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right]$$

Term 3:

$$-\frac{1}{2}(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right](\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)$$

The full difference is:

$$Q(\hat{\theta}|\tilde{\theta}) - \ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}) = \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right]$$ $$+ (\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right]$$ $$- \frac{1}{2}(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right](\hat{\theta}-\theta^*) + O_p(N^{-3/2})$$

Step 9: Taking the Full Expectation

Taking expectations over the joint distribution of $(Y^{obs}, Y^{mis})$ from $p(Y|\theta^*)$:

$$\text{Bias} = \mathbb{E}[Q(\hat{\theta}|\tilde{\theta}) - \ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})]$$

This involves expectations of all three terms. We will analyze each term separately.

For Term 1, the expectation is:

$$\mathbb{E}\left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right]$$

Note that $\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)$ on the right is a random variable depending on the true $Y^{mis} \sim p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*)$.

For Term 2:

$$\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right]\right]$$

For Term 3:

$$-\frac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right](\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)\right]$$

Step 10: Analyzing Term 1

We can rewrite Term 1 by introducing the expectation under the true distribution:

$$\mathbb{E}\left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right]$$

The outer expectation is over $(Y^{obs}, Y^{mis}) \sim p(Y|\theta^*)$. We can write this as:

$$= \mathbb{E}_{Y^{obs}}\left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]\right]$$

This is the difference in the expected log-likelihood of the missing data when imputing under $\tilde{\theta}$ versus under the true $\theta^*$.

Step 11: Evaluating the Difference in Conditional Expectations

We need to evaluate:

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]$$

Taylor expand the first term as a function of the conditioning parameter $\tilde{\theta}$ around $\theta^*$:

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] \approx \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] + (\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \frac{\partial}{\partial\tilde{\theta}}\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]\bigg|_{\tilde{\theta}=\theta^*}$$

For the derivative, we use the score function identity. If $g(Y^{mis})$ is any function:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial\tilde{\theta}}\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[g(Y^{mis})] = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}\left[g(Y^{mis}) \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial\tilde{\theta}}\log p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta})\right]$$

Since $\frac{\partial}{\partial\tilde{\theta}}\log p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}) = S_{mis|obs}(\tilde{\theta})$, we have:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial\tilde{\theta}}\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]\bigg|_{\tilde{\theta}=\theta^*} = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}\left[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right]$$

Therefore:

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]$$ $$\approx (\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}\left[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right]$$

Step 12: Term 1 is Zero (to first order)

We now show that $\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}\left[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right] = 0$.

Using integration by parts (or the product rule for differentiation under the integral sign):

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}\left[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right] = \int \ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*) \, dY^{mis}$$

Note that $S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) = \partial_\theta \log p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*) = \frac{\partial_\theta p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*)}{p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*)}$.

So:

$$= \int \ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \partial_\theta p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*) \, dY^{mis}$$

Integrating by parts (with boundary terms vanishing under regularity conditions):

$$= -\int \partial_{Y^{mis}}\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*) \, dY^{mis} \cdot (\text{something})$$

Actually, a more direct approach: Note that $\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) = \log p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*)$, so:

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}\left[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right] = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}\left[\log p \cdot \frac{\partial_\theta p}{p}\right]$$ $$= \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}\left[\frac{\partial_\theta p \cdot \log p}{p}\right]$$

By the identity $\partial_\theta \mathbb{E}[g] = \mathbb{E}[g \cdot S]$, and setting $g = \ell_{mis|obs}$:

$$\partial_\theta \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]\bigg|_{\theta=\theta^*} = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]$$

But $\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]$ is the cross-entropy between $p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta)$ and $p(Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*)$. This is minimized when $\theta = \theta^*$ (where it equals the negative entropy), so its derivative at $\theta = \theta^*$ is zero:

$$\partial_\theta \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta}[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]\bigg|_{\theta=\theta^*} = 0$$

Therefore:

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}\left[\ell_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right] = 0$$

This means Term 1 contributes only $O((\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)^2) = O(N^{-1})$ from higher-order terms in the Taylor expansion.

Step 13: Analyzing Term 2

Term 2 is:

$$\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right]\right]$$

We analyze the inner bracket. First, note that:

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] = 0$$

This is the standard result that the expected score is zero at the true parameter.

Taylor expand $\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]$ around $\tilde{\theta} = \theta^*$:

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] \approx \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] + (\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \frac{\partial}{\partial\tilde{\theta}}\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]\bigg|_{\tilde{\theta}=\theta^*}$$

The first term is zero. For the derivative, using the score function identity:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial\tilde{\theta}}\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]\bigg|_{\tilde{\theta}=\theta^*} = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}\left[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)^T\right]$$

This is the Fisher information for the missing data conditional on observed data:

$$\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}\left[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)^T\right]$$

Therefore:

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] \approx \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)$$

Step 14: Substituting Back into Term 2

Substituting the result from Step 13 into Term 2:

$$\text{Term 2} = \mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*) - S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right]\right]$$

Split this into two parts:

$$= \mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)\right] - \mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right]$$

For the second part, we use iterated expectations:

$$\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right] = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{obs}}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)]\right]$$

Since $\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] = 0$, this term vanishes:

$$\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T S_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right] = 0$$

Therefore:

$$\text{Term 2} = \mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)\right]$$

Step 15: Term 3 is Higher Order

Term 3 is:

$$-\frac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)\right](\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)\right]$$

The bracket $[\cdots]$ involves the difference between the expected observed information under the imputation distribution and the actual observed information. Taylor expanding:

$$\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] - \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[\mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] = O(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*) = O_p(N^{-1/2})$$

And:

$$\mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) - \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\theta^*}[\mathcal{J}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)] = O_p(N^{-1/2})$$

So the bracket is $O_p(N^{-1/2})$.

This is multiplied by $(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T[\cdot](\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)$, which is $O_p(N^{-1})$.

Therefore:

$$\text{Term 3} = O_p(N^{-3/2})$$

Combining all terms:

$$\text{Bias} = \underbrace{O(N^{-1})}_{\text{Term 1}} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)\right]}_{\text{Term 2}} + \underbrace{O(N^{-3/2})}_{\text{Term 3}}$$

The leading-order bias is from Term 2:

Leading-order bias: $$\text{Bias} = \mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)\right] + O(N^{-1})$$

Step 16: Converting to Trace Form

For any vectors $a, c$ and matrix $B$, we have the identity:

$$a^T B c = \text{tr}(B c a^T)$$

Applying this with $a = \hat{\theta}-\theta^*$, $c = \tilde{\theta}-\theta^*$, and $B = \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)$:

$$(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*) = \text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T\right)$$

Taking expectations and using linearity of trace and expectation:

$$\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)\right] = \text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \mathbb{E}\left[(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T\right]\right)$$

Assuming both $\tilde{\theta}$ and $\hat{\theta}$ are asymptotically unbiased for $\theta^*$ (i.e., $\mathbb{E}[\tilde{\theta}] = \theta^* + o(1)$ and $\mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}] = \theta^* + o(1)$), we have:

$$\mathbb{E}\left[(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)(\hat{\theta}-\theta^*)^T\right] = \text{Cov}(\tilde{\theta}, \hat{\theta}) + o(N^{-1})$$

Therefore:

Final Result: $$\boxed{\text{Bias} = \text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \text{Cov}(\tilde{\theta}, \hat{\theta})\right) + O(N^{-1})}$$

Special Case: MLE Imputation ($\tilde{\theta} = \hat{\theta}$)

When the same MLE is used for both imputation and evaluation, $\tilde{\theta} = \hat{\theta}$, so:

$$\text{Cov}(\tilde{\theta}, \hat{\theta}) = \text{Cov}(\hat{\theta}, \hat{\theta}) = \text{Var}(\hat{\theta})$$

where $\hat{\theta} = \hat{\theta}_{obs} = \arg\max_\theta \ell_{obs}(\theta|Y^{obs})$ is the MLE from the observed-data likelihood.

Important distinction: $\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})$ is the variance of the MLE from the observed-data likelihood, not the variance we would have from maximizing the complete-data likelihood. Under standard regularity conditions: $$\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}) = \mathcal{I}_{obs}(\theta^*)^{-1}/N + o(N^{-1})$$ where $\mathcal{I}_{obs}(\theta^*) = \mathbb{E}[-\partial_\theta^2 \ell_{obs}(\theta^*)]$ is the observed-data Fisher information.

Substituting:

$$\text{Bias} = \text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})\right) + O(N^{-1})$$
$$= \text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}(\theta^*)^{-1}\right)/N + O(N^{-1})$$
MLE Imputation Bias: $$\boxed{\text{Bias} = \frac{1}{N}\text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}(\theta^*)^{-1}\right) + O(N^{-1})}$$

Why the Bias is Positive

The bias $\text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \text{Cov}(\tilde{\theta}, \hat{\theta})\right)$ is non-negative because:

  1. $\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)$ is positive semi-definite: It is a Fisher information matrix, defined as $\mathbb{E}[S_{mis|obs} S_{mis|obs}^T]$, which is always p.s.d.
  2. $\text{Cov}(\tilde{\theta}, \hat{\theta})$ is positive semi-definite: For the MLE case, this is $\text{Var}(\hat{\theta})$, which is a covariance matrix and hence p.s.d. More generally, $\text{Cov}(\tilde{\theta}, \hat{\theta})$ can be shown to be p.s.d. when $\tilde{\theta}$ and $\hat{\theta}$ are both functions of the same data $Y^{obs}$.
  3. Trace of product of p.s.d. matrices is non-negative: For any p.s.d. matrices $A$ and $B$, $\text{tr}(AB) \geq 0$.

The bias is strictly positive when:

  • There is genuine parameter uncertainty: $\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) \neq 0$
  • There is missing information: $\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \neq 0$
  • The uncertainty is in directions that have missing information (the matrices share non-trivial intersection of their column spaces)

Can the Bias Be Corrected?

The bias formula requires $\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)$—the missing-data Fisher information matrix evaluated at the true parameter $\theta^*$.

Since $\theta^*$ is unknown, we must estimate this quantity. Using $\hat{\mathcal{I}}_{mis|obs}(\hat{\theta})$ computed from imputations at $\hat{\theta}$:

$$\mathbb{E}[\hat{\mathcal{I}}_{mis|obs}(\hat{\theta})] = \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) + O(N^{-1})$$

This is unbiased to leading order. Therefore, the bias can in principle be corrected.

However, practical challenges remain:

  • Computing $\hat{\mathcal{I}}_{mis|obs}(\hat{\theta})$ requires generating imputations and estimating the information matrix—additional computational burden
  • Existing methods (AICcd, standard MI) do not include this correction
  • The correction must be applied twice (once for imputation bias, once for optimism), which current methods do not do

Conclusion: Targeting complete-data divergence is possible but requires more complex corrections than existing methods provide. This motivates observed-data targets as a simpler alternative.

Summary

We have shown that when imputation is used to estimate the complete-data log-likelihood:

$$Q(\hat{\theta}|\tilde{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})]$$

the bias relative to the true complete-data log-likelihood is:

$$\text{Bias} = \mathbb{E}[Q(\hat{\theta}|\tilde{\theta})] - \mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})] = \text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \text{Cov}(\tilde{\theta}, \hat{\theta})\right) + O(N^{-1})$$

For MLE imputation ($\tilde{\theta} = \hat{\theta}$):

$$\text{Bias} = \frac{1}{N}\text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}(\theta^*)^{-1}\right) + O(N^{-1})$$

This bias is:

  • Positive: Models appear to fit better than they truly do
  • Unavoidable: Arises whenever $\tilde{\theta}$ is estimated from data
  • Correctable in principle: $\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)$ can be estimated to leading order, but existing methods do not include this correction

This result shows that existing imputation-based criteria (such as AICcd) underpenalize, motivating either corrected penalties for complete-data targets or—more simply—observed-data targets with entropy-equivalent evaluation.

3. Consequences for Model Comparison

We now examine how the bias affects comparisons between models of different complexity, showing that the bias scales linearly with the number of parameters and systematically favors more complex models.

Setup for Model Comparison

Consider comparing two nested or non-nested models:

  • Model A: $Q_A$ parameters
  • Model B: $Q_B > Q_A$ parameters (more complex)

For each model, the bias in using the Q-function to estimate the complete-data log-likelihood is (for the MLE imputation case):

$$\text{Bias}_A = \frac{1}{N}\text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}^{(A)}(\theta^*_A) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{(A)}(\theta^*_A)^{-1}\right)$$
$$\text{Bias}_B = \frac{1}{N}\text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}^{(B)}(\theta^*_B) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{(B)}(\theta^*_B)^{-1}\right)$$

How the Trace Scales with Model Complexity

The trace $\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$ can be understood in terms of eigenvalues. Working in an appropriate basis:

$$\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}) = \sum_{j=1}^{Q} \frac{\lambda_j^{(mis)}}{\lambda_j^{(obs)}}$$

where $\lambda_j^{(mis)}$ and $\lambda_j^{(obs)}$ are the eigenvalues of the missing-data and observed-data information matrices in the $j$-th parameter direction.

The Missing Information Fraction

Each ratio $\frac{\lambda_j^{(mis)}}{\lambda_j^{(obs)}}$ has a natural interpretation. Define the fraction of missing information for parameter direction $j$:

$$\rho_j = \frac{\lambda_j^{(mis)}}{\lambda_j^{(obs)} + \lambda_j^{(mis)}} = \frac{\lambda_j^{(mis)}}{\lambda_j^{(com)}}$$

This is the proportion of total (complete-data) information that is lost due to missingness. Then:

$$\frac{\lambda_j^{(mis)}}{\lambda_j^{(obs)}} = \frac{\rho_j}{1 - \rho_j}$$

When $\rho_j = 0$ (no missing information), the ratio is 0. When $\rho_j \to 1$ (nearly all information missing), the ratio diverges.

Linear Scaling with Model Complexity

For a model with $Q$ parameters, the bias is:

$$\text{Bias} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{j=1}^{Q} \frac{\lambda_j^{(mis)}}{\lambda_j^{(obs)}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{j=1}^{Q} \frac{\rho_j}{1 - \rho_j}$$

If on average each parameter direction has missing information fraction $\bar{\rho}$, then:

$$\text{Bias} \approx \frac{Q}{N} \cdot \frac{\bar{\rho}}{1 - \bar{\rho}}$$

The bias scales linearly with $Q$ (model complexity).

Differential Bias Between Models

When comparing Model B (complex, $Q_B$ parameters) to Model A (simple, $Q_A$ parameters):

$$\text{Bias}_B - \text{Bias}_A \approx \frac{Q_B - Q_A}{N} \cdot \frac{\bar{\rho}}{1 - \bar{\rho}}$$

This differential bias is positive, meaning:

The more complex model receives a larger artificial boost.
  • Both models have positive bias (both appear to fit better than they truly do)
  • The complex model has more positive bias because it has more parameters
  • When comparing $Q(\hat{\theta}_B|\hat{\theta}_B) - Q(\hat{\theta}_A|\hat{\theta}_A)$, the complex model gets an artificial advantage of approximately $\frac{Q_B - Q_A}{N} \cdot \frac{\bar{\rho}}{1 - \bar{\rho}}$

Effect of Missing Data Rate

The factor $\frac{\bar{\rho}}{1 - \bar{\rho}}$ amplifies the bias as missing data increases:

Missing Info Fraction ($\bar{\rho}$) Multiplier $\frac{\bar{\rho}}{1-\bar{\rho}}$ Interpretation
10% 0.11 Modest bias
25% 0.33 Moderate bias
50% 1.00 Bias equals $Q/N$
75% 3.00 Severe bias
90% 9.00 Extreme bias

With substantial missing data ($\bar{\rho} \geq 0.5$), the bias per parameter can equal or exceed the standard AIC penalty of $2/N$ per parameter.

Consequences for Model Selection

If researchers use Q-function estimates or MI-averaged complete-data log-likelihoods for model selection:

  1. All models appear to fit better than they do: The positive bias inflates the apparent fit of every model.
  2. Complex models get a larger artificial boost: Since bias $\propto Q$, adding parameters artificially improves apparent fit beyond the true improvement.
  3. Net effect: Systematic preference for overly complex models (overfitting).
Contrast with FIML + Standard Penalties:

This is the opposite of the problem with FIML estimation using standard AIC/BIC penalties:

Approach Problem Direction of Error
FIML + standard penalty Overpenalization (penalty assumes all $N$ observations inform all $Q$ parameters) Favors parsimony (underfitting)
Imputation-based Positive bias in deviance scaling with $Q$ Favors complexity (overfitting)

Both approaches are incongruous—they fail to properly account for the information structure of incomplete data—but they err in opposite directions.

Summary: Scaling of Bias

Quantity Scaling Effect on Model Selection
Bias per model $\propto Q/N$ All models appear to fit better
Differential bias (B vs A) $\propto (Q_B - Q_A)/N$ Complex model artificially favored
Missing data effect $\propto \bar{\rho}/(1-\bar{\rho})$ More missing data → larger bias

This analysis demonstrates that imputation-based approaches to model selection not only have unavoidable bias (Section 2), but that this bias systematically distorts model comparisons in favor of complexity. Combined with the impossibility of correcting the bias (which requires the unobservable $\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)$), this motivates abandoning complete-data targets entirely in favor of observed-data targets that respect entropy conservation.

4. Relationship to Shimodaira's PDIO

The trace term $\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$ appears in both our derivation and in Shimodaira's (1994) Predictive Divergence for Incomplete Observations (PDIO). This section clarifies how these results relate and why they address different problems.

Shimodaira's Setup

Shimodaira asked: If I use the observed-data deviance $-2\ell_{obs}(\hat{\theta})$, what penalty do I need to target complete-data KL divergence?

The target is complete-data divergence:

$$\text{KL}_{com} = \mathbb{E}_{Y^{com}}\left[\log f_{true}(Y^{com}) - \log f(Y^{com}|\theta)\right]$$

The estimator uses the observed-data log-likelihood $\ell_{obs}(\hat{\theta})$ as the measure of fit.

Shimodaira's Derivation

The standard AIC derivation shows that the bias in using the empirical log-likelihood to estimate expected log-likelihood is:

$$\text{Bias} \approx \frac{1}{N}\text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{target} \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{\theta})\right)$$

where $\mathcal{I}_{target}$ is the Fisher information for the target likelihood.

In Shimodaira's situation:

  • Target: Complete-data likelihood, with curvature $\mathcal{I}_{com}$
  • Estimator variance: From observed-data likelihood, $\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}) = \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}/N$

Therefore:

$$\text{Bias} \approx \frac{1}{N}\text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}\right)$$

This gives the PDIO criterion:

$$\text{PDIO} = -2\ell_{obs}(\hat{\theta}) + 2\text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}\right)$$

Decomposing the PDIO Penalty

Using $\mathcal{I}_{com} = \mathcal{I}_{obs} + \mathcal{I}_{mis}$:

$$\text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}\right) = \text{tr}\left((\mathcal{I}_{obs} + \mathcal{I}_{mis}) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}\right) = \text{tr}(I_Q) + \text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}\right)$$
$$= Q + \text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}\right)$$

So the PDIO penalty is:

$$2\text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}\right) = 2Q + 2\text{tr}\left(\mathcal{I}_{mis} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}\right)$$

This consists of:

  • $2Q$: The standard AIC penalty
  • $2\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$: An adjustment for the mismatch between complete-data target and observed-data estimation

Our Derivation vs. Shimodaira's

The key distinction is what deviance term is being used:

Approach Deviance Term Target What Needs Correction
Shimodaira (PDIO) $-2\ell_{obs}(\hat{\theta})$ (observed-data) $\text{KL}_{com}$ Penalty term (to bridge obs→com)
Our derivation $-2Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})$ (imputed complete-data) $\text{KL}_{com}$ Bias in deviance itself
Shimodaira's deviance is unbiased. The observed-data log-likelihood $\ell_{obs}(\hat{\theta})$ evaluated at the MLE is a valid (though optimistic) measure of fit to the observed data. The PDIO penalty corrects for optimism and bridges to the complete-data target. The Q-function deviance is biased. Using $Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})$ as a proxy for $\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})$ introduces bias because imputation conditions on $\hat{\theta}$ rather than $\theta^*$. This bias exists before any penalty is applied.

Why the Same Trace Appears

The trace $\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$ appears in both derivations, but for different reasons:

In Shimodaira's derivation:

  • It captures the mismatch between target (complete-data) and estimation (observed-data)
  • The penalty accounts for predicting complete-data fit using parameters estimated from observed data
  • It's a penalty correction for targeting $\text{KL}_{com}$ with $\ell_{obs}$

In our derivation:

  • It captures the artificial coherence from imputing at $\hat{\theta}$ instead of $\theta^*$
  • The bias arises because $\hat{\theta}$ is correlated with $Y^{obs}$, and imputation propagates this correlation
  • It's a deviance bias in using $Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})$ to estimate $\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})$

The mathematical coincidence arises because both phenomena involve the interaction between missing-data information structure ($\mathcal{I}_{mis}$) and observed-data estimation uncertainty ($\mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}$).

Implications for Practice

PDIO uses the correct deviance but requires estimating $\mathcal{I}_{com}$.

Shimodaira's approach uses $\ell_{obs}(\hat{\theta})$ (unbiased) but requires computing $\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$. Estimating $\mathcal{I}_{com}$ adds computational complexity and is not implemented in standard software.

Imputation-based approaches have biased deviance and incomplete penalties.

If one uses $Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})$ as the deviance:

  1. The deviance is biased by $\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$ (our result)
  2. Existing penalties (e.g., AICcd) correct for optimism but not for imputation bias
  3. The correct penalty requires twice the $\mathcal{I}_{mis}$ adjustment of current methods

Summary: Two Routes, Same Challenge

Both Shimodaira's PDIO and imputation-based approaches attempt to target complete-data divergence. Both require estimating or correcting for terms involving $\mathcal{I}_{mis}(\theta^*)$.

  • PDIO: Correct deviance ($\ell_{obs}$), but penalty requires estimating $\mathcal{I}_{com}(\theta^*)$
  • Imputation: Biased deviance ($Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})$), requiring additional correction for imputation bias

While $\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*)$ can be estimated to leading order, existing methods do not include the full correction. The appearance of $\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$ in both derivations reflects the additional complexity of targeting complete-data quantities with incomplete data. Observed-data targets sidestep this complexity entirely.

5. Cavanaugh & Shumway's AICcd Underpenalizes

Cavanaugh and Shumway (1998) proposed AICcd, which uses the Q-function as its deviance term. We show that their criterion underpenalizes because it does not account for the imputation bias derived in Section 2.

Step 1: The Target

AIC-type criteria target expected predictive performance on new data. For complete-data model selection, the target is:

$$\mathbb{E}[\bar{\ell}_{com}(\hat{\theta})]$$

where $\bar{\ell}_{com}(\hat{\theta})$ is the expected complete-data log-likelihood under a new independent sample from $p(\cdot|\theta^*)$.

Step 2: The Total Bias

The total bias in using $Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})$ to estimate the target is:

$$\text{Total Bias} = \mathbb{E}[Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})] - \mathbb{E}[\bar{\ell}_{com}(\hat{\theta})]$$

We decompose this by inserting $\mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})]$:

$$\text{Total Bias} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})] - \mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})]}_{\text{Imputation Bias}} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})] - \mathbb{E}[\bar{\ell}_{com}(\hat{\theta})]}_{\text{Optimism}}$$

Step 3: Evaluating Each Term

Imputation Bias (from Section 2):

$$\mathbb{E}[Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})] - \mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})] = \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}))$$

Optimism (standard AIC-type result):

$$\mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})] - \mathbb{E}[\bar{\ell}_{com}(\hat{\theta})] = \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{com}(\theta^*) \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}))$$

Total Bias:

$$\text{Total Bias} = \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})) + \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{com}(\theta^*) \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}))$$
$$= \text{tr}\left((\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} + \mathcal{I}_{com}) \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})\right)$$

Using $\mathcal{I}_{com} = \mathcal{I}_{obs} + \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}$:

$$\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} + \mathcal{I}_{com} = \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} + \mathcal{I}_{obs} + \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} = \mathcal{I}_{obs} + 2\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}$$

And since $\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}) = \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}$:

$$\text{Total Bias} = \text{tr}\left((\mathcal{I}_{obs} + 2\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}\right)$$
$$= \text{tr}(I_Q) + 2\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$$
$$\text{Total Bias} = Q + 2\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$$

Step 4: What Cavanaugh & Shumway's Penalty Corrects

Their AICcd is:

$$\text{AICcd} = -2Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta}) + 2\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$$

Their penalty is $2\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$. Using $\mathcal{I}_{com} = \mathcal{I}_{obs} + \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}$:

$$2\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}) = 2\text{tr}((\mathcal{I}_{obs} + \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}) = 2Q + 2\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$$

The correct penalty should be:

$$2 \times \text{Total Bias} = 2Q + 4\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$$

Step 5: The Discrepancy

Cavanaugh & Shumway underpenalize by $2\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$.

Their penalty corrects for the optimism bias but not the imputation bias. The missing penalty term scales with both model complexity and missing information.

Summary

Criterion Penalty What it corrects
Cavanaugh & Shumway AICcd $2Q + 2\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$ Optimism only
Correct penalty (targeting complete-data) $2Q + 4\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$ Optimism + Imputation bias

Consequence: AICcd underpenalizes by $2\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$, which scales with the number of parameters and the missing information fraction. This causes AICcd to favor overly complex models.

While the correct penalty ($2Q + 4\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$) can in principle be estimated, existing methods do not implement this correction. This motivates either developing corrected complete-data criteria or—more simply—targeting observed-data divergence, which avoids the need for these corrections entirely.

6. MI-AIC: Corrected AIC for Proper Multiple Imputation

We derive a corrected AIC for model selection when using proper multiple imputation. Unlike AICcd (which uses the EM Q-function), this approach uses precomputed proper imputations and expresses the correction in terms of standard MI output.

Step 1: Setup

We have precomputed proper imputations:

  • For $m = 1, \ldots, M$: draw $\tilde{\theta}^{(m)} \sim p(\theta | Y^{obs})$
  • For each $m$: impute $\tilde{Y}^{mis,(m)} \sim p(Y^{mis} | Y^{obs}, \tilde{\theta}^{(m)})$

For a candidate model, compute the MLE on observed data:

$$\hat{\theta}_{obs} = \arg\max_\theta \ell_{obs}(\theta | Y^{obs})$$

Evaluate the average complete-data log-likelihood:

$$\bar{Q}_{MI}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}) = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^{M} \ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}_{obs} | Y^{obs}, \tilde{Y}^{mis,(m)})$$

As $M \to \infty$:

$$\bar{Q}_{MI}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}) \to \mathbb{E}_{\tilde{\theta} \sim p(\theta|Y^{obs})}\left[\mathbb{E}_{Y^{mis}|Y^{obs},\tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})]\right]$$

Target: $\mathbb{E}[\bar{\ell}_{com}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})]$ (expected complete-data log-likelihood on new data)

Step 2: The Total Bias

$$\text{Total Bias} = \mathbb{E}[\bar{Q}_{MI}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})] - \mathbb{E}[\bar{\ell}_{com}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})]$$

Decomposing:

$$\text{Total Bias} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[\bar{Q}_{MI}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})] - \mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})]}_{\text{Imputation Bias}} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})] - \mathbb{E}[\bar{\ell}_{com}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})]}_{\text{Optimism}}$$

Step 3: Evaluating the Imputation Bias

From our earlier derivation (Section 2):

$$\text{Imputation Bias} = \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \mathbb{E}[(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)(\hat{\theta}_{obs}-\theta^*)^T])$$

Write $\tilde{\theta} = \bar{\theta}(Y^{obs}) + \epsilon$ where $\bar{\theta}$ is the posterior mean and $\mathbb{E}[\epsilon|Y^{obs}] = 0$.

Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)(\hat{\theta}_{obs}-\theta^*)^T] = \mathbb{E}[(\bar{\theta}-\theta^*)(\hat{\theta}_{obs}-\theta^*)^T] + \mathbb{E}[\epsilon(\hat{\theta}_{obs}-\theta^*)^T]$$

The second term vanishes by iterated expectations:

$$\mathbb{E}[\epsilon(\hat{\theta}_{obs}-\theta^*)^T] = \mathbb{E}\left[\mathbb{E}[\epsilon|Y^{obs}](\hat{\theta}_{obs}-\theta^*)^T\right] = 0$$

Asymptotically, $\bar{\theta} \approx \hat{\theta}_{obs}$, so:

$$\mathbb{E}[(\tilde{\theta}-\theta^*)(\hat{\theta}_{obs}-\theta^*)^T] \approx \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}) = \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}$$

Therefore:

$$\text{Imputation Bias} = \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$$

Step 4: Evaluating the Optimism

The optimism is:

$$\text{Optimism} = \mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})] - \mathbb{E}[\bar{\ell}_{com}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})]$$

The standard result is:

$$\text{Optimism} = \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{target} \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}))$$

Here:

  • Target is complete-data likelihood: $\mathcal{I}_{target} = \mathcal{I}_{com}$
  • Estimator is $\hat{\theta}_{obs}$: $\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}) = \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}$

Therefore:

$$\text{Optimism} = \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$$

Step 5: Total Bias

Combining the imputation bias and optimism:

$$\text{Total Bias} = \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}) + \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$$
$$= \text{tr}((\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} + \mathcal{I}_{com}) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$$

Using $\mathcal{I}_{com} = \mathcal{I}_{obs} + \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}$:

$$= \text{tr}((\mathcal{I}_{obs} + 2\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$$
$$\text{Total Bias} = Q + 2\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$$

Step 6: Expressing in Estimable Quantities

Starting from:

$$\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1} = (\mathcal{I}_{com} - \mathcal{I}_{obs}) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1} = \mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1} - I_Q$$

Using the variance relationships from Rubin's rules:

  • $\mathcal{I}_{com}^{-1} \approx W$ (within-imputation variance)
  • $\mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1} \approx T = W + (1 + 1/M)B$ (total variance)

So:

$$\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1} = W^{-1}T = W^{-1}(W + (1 + 1/M)B) = I_Q + (1 + 1/M)W^{-1}B$$

Therefore:

$$\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1} = (1 + 1/M)W^{-1}B = \text{RIV}$$

where $\text{RIV} = (1 + 1/M)W^{-1}B$ is the relative increase in variance matrix, directly computable from standard MI output. The factor $(1 + 1/M)$ accounts for finite $M$; as $M \to \infty$, it converges to 1.

And:

$$\text{Total Bias} = Q + 2\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$$

The correct penalty is:

$$2 \times \text{Total Bias} = 2Q + 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$$

MI-AIC Formula

MI-AIC (Corrected AIC for Proper Multiple Imputation): $$\boxed{\text{MI-AIC} = -2\bar{Q}_{MI}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}) + 2Q + 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV})}$$

where:

  • $\bar{Q}_{MI}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}) = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^{M} \ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}_{obs} | Y^{obs}, \tilde{Y}^{mis,(m)})$
  • $Q$ = number of parameters
  • $\text{RIV} = (1 + 1/M)W^{-1}B$ is the relative increase in variance matrix
  • $W$ = within-imputation variance, $B$ = between-imputation variance, $M$ = number of imputations

Comparison to Standard AIC

Criterion Penalty
Standard AIC (complete data) $2Q$
MI-AIC (proper MI, targeting complete-data divergence) $2Q + 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$

Practical Notes

Computing MI-AIC:
  1. Generate proper imputations $\tilde{Y}^{mis,(m)}$ for $m = 1, \ldots, M$
  2. Fit the candidate model to observed data to obtain $\hat{\theta}_{obs}$
  3. Evaluate $\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}_{obs})$ on each completed dataset and average
  4. Compute $W$ (average of complete-data variance estimates) and $B$ (variance of point estimates across imputations)
  5. Calculate $\text{tr}(\text{RIV}) = \text{tr}((1 + 1/M)W^{-1}B)$
  6. Apply the formula: $\text{MI-AIC} = -2\bar{Q}_{MI} + 2Q + 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$

The key advantage of MI-AIC is that the correction term $\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$ is directly available from standard MI output, making it straightforward to implement in practice.

7. Generalization to Missing Covariates

The derivations in Sections 1–6 used $Y = (Y^{obs}, Y^{mis})$ notation, which is natural for missing responses. Here we generalize to handle any pattern of missingness in both responses and covariates.

Step 1: General Setup

Complete data: $Z = (Y, X)$ where $Y$ is the response vector and $X$ is the covariate matrix.

Partition by observability: $Z = (Z^{obs}, Z^{mis})$, where $Z^{mis}$ may include components of $Y$, $X$, or both.

Factorization: Assume the joint density factors as:

$$f(Z; \theta) = f(Y|X; \beta) \cdot f(X; \alpha)$$

with $\theta = (\beta, \alpha)$.

Assumption (Distinct parameters): $\beta$ and $\alpha$ are a priori independent, meaning:
  • $\beta \in \mathcal{B}$, $\alpha \in \mathcal{A}$, with $\theta \in \mathcal{B} \times \mathcal{A}$
  • No functional constraints linking $\beta$ and $\alpha$
This is a standard assumption in missing data problems (e.g., Claeskens & Consentino, 2008).

Step 2: Decomposition of Complete-Data Log-Likelihood

The complete-data log-likelihood decomposes:

$$\ell_{com}(\theta) = \underbrace{\sum_i \log f(y_i | x_i; \beta)}_{\ell_{Y|X}(\beta)} + \underbrace{\sum_i \log f(x_i; \alpha)}_{\ell_X(\alpha)}$$

Step 3: Q-Function Decomposition

The Q-function is:

$$Q(\theta|\tilde{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{Z^{mis}|Z^{obs}, \tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{com}(\theta)]$$

By linearity of expectation:

$$Q(\theta|\tilde{\theta}) = Q_{Y|X}(\beta|\tilde{\theta}) + Q_X(\alpha|\tilde{\theta})$$

where:

  • $Q_{Y|X}(\beta|\tilde{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{Z^{mis}|Z^{obs}, \tilde{\theta}}[\ell_{Y|X}(\beta)]$
  • $Q_X(\alpha|\tilde{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{Z^{mis}|Z^{obs}, \tilde{\theta}}[\ell_X(\alpha)]$

Step 4: Total Bias for the Joint Model

From Section 6, the total bias when using $Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})$ to target $\mathbb{E}[\bar{\ell}_{com}(\hat{\theta})]$ is:

$$\text{Total Bias} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})] - \mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})]}_{\text{Imputation bias}} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})] - \mathbb{E}[\bar{\ell}_{com}(\hat{\theta})]}_{\text{Optimism}}$$

With:

  • Imputation bias $= \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta^*) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}(\theta^*))$
  • Optimism $= \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{com}(\theta^*) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}(\theta^*))$

Step 5: Block Diagonal Structure

Under the distinct parameters assumption, the information matrices are block diagonal:

$$\mathcal{I}_{com}(\theta) = \begin{pmatrix} \mathcal{I}_{com,\beta} & 0 \\ 0 & \mathcal{I}_{com,\alpha} \end{pmatrix}$$
$$\mathcal{I}_{obs}(\theta) = \begin{pmatrix} \mathcal{I}_{obs,\beta} & 0 \\ 0 & \mathcal{I}_{obs,\alpha} \end{pmatrix}$$
$$\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs}(\theta) = \begin{pmatrix} \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs,\beta} & 0 \\ 0 & \mathcal{I}_{mis|obs,\alpha} \end{pmatrix}$$

Therefore the traces separate:

$$\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}) = \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs,\beta} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs,\beta}^{-1}) + \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs,\alpha} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs,\alpha}^{-1})$$
$$\text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}) = \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{com,\beta} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs,\beta}^{-1}) + \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{com,\alpha} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs,\alpha}^{-1})$$

Step 6: MI-AIC for the Joint Model

The MI-AIC for the joint model is:

$$\text{MI-AIC}_{Y,X} = -2Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta}) + 2Q_\beta + 2Q_\alpha + 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV}_\beta) + 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV}_\alpha)$$

Using the Q-function decomposition and grouping by component:

$$= \underbrace{\left[-2Q_{Y|X}(\hat{\beta}|\hat{\theta}) + 2Q_\beta + 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV}_\beta)\right]}_{\text{MI-AIC}_{Y|X}} + \underbrace{\left[-2Q_X(\hat{\alpha}|\hat{\theta}) + 2Q_\alpha + 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV}_\alpha)\right]}_{\text{MI-AIC}_X}$$

Step 7: MI-AIC for the Conditional Model

For model selection in regression, we typically care only about the conditional model $f(Y|X;\beta)$. The relevant criterion is:

MI-AIC for conditional model: $$\boxed{\text{MI-AIC}_{Y|X} = -2Q_{Y|X}(\hat{\beta}|\hat{\theta}) + 2Q_\beta + 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV}_\beta)}$$

This equals:

$$\text{MI-AIC}_{Y|X} = \text{MI-AIC}_{Y,X} - \text{MI-AIC}_X$$

where:

  • $Q_{Y|X}(\hat{\beta}|\hat{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{Z^{mis}|Z^{obs},\hat{\theta}}[\ell_{Y|X}(\hat{\beta})]$
  • $Q_\beta$ = number of regression parameters
  • $\text{RIV}_\beta = W_\beta^{-1} B_\beta$ = relative increase in variance for $\beta$

8. Comparison to Claeskens & Consentino (2008)

Claeskens & Consentino (2008) propose model selection criteria for missing covariate problems. We now compare their approach to MI-AIC.

Their Setup

They model the complete data as $f(Y, X; \theta) = f(Y|X; \beta) \cdot f(X; \alpha)$ with the same distinct parameters assumption. They use the Q-function from the EM algorithm and define:

  • $I(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\{-\ddot{Q}(\theta|\theta)/n\}$ — expected negative Hessian of Q
  • $J(\theta) = \text{Var}\{\dot{Q}(\theta|\theta)\}/n$ — variance of Q's score

Their Criteria

TIC (model-robust):

$$\text{TIC} = 2Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta}) - 2\text{tr}(\hat{J}\hat{I}^{-1})$$

AIC (when model correct, $I = J$):

$$\text{AIC} = 2Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta}) - 2 \cdot \text{length}(\theta)$$

AIC₁ (for regression part only):

$$\text{AIC}_1 = 2Q^{(1)}(\hat{\beta}|\hat{\theta}) - 2Q_\beta$$

What They Target

Critically, Claeskens & Consentino redefine the target. From their paper (p. 6):

"When using the integrated weighted log likelihood function instead of $\log f_\theta$, the relevant Kullback-Leibler distance to minimize is $KL(g, \tilde{f}_\theta)$"

where $\tilde{f}_\theta = \exp Q(\theta|\theta)$. Their target is Q, not $\ell_{com}$.

Their Theorem 1 derives:

$$\mathbb{E}(\hat{K}_n - K_n) = \text{tr}(I^{-1}J)/n$$

This is the bias for using $Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})$ to predict Q on new data—not to predict $\ell_{com}$ on new data.

The Gap

If the goal is to replicate complete-data model selection (which targets $\ell_{com}$), there is an additional bias they don't account for:

$$\text{Imputation Bias} = \mathbb{E}[Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})] - \mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})] = \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1})$$

Comparing their AIC₁ to our MI-AIC$_{Y|X}$:

Criterion Formula Penalty
C&C AIC₁ $2Q_{Y|X}(\hat{\beta}|\hat{\theta}) - 2Q_\beta$ $2Q_\beta$
MI-AIC$_{Y|X}$ $-2Q_{Y|X}(\hat{\beta}|\hat{\theta}) + 2Q_\beta + 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV}_\beta)$ $2Q_\beta + 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV}_\beta)$

The gap:

$$\text{MI-AIC}_{Y|X} - (-\text{AIC}_1) = 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV}_\beta)$$

C&C's AIC₁ underpenalizes by $4\text{tr}(\text{RIV}_\beta)$—the doubled imputation bias correction for the regression parameters—because they target Q rather than $\ell_{com}$.

Summary of Existing Methods

Paper Target Claimed Imputation Bias Addressed?
Cavanaugh & Shumway (AICcd) Complete-data divergence No (underpenalizes)
Shimodaira (PDIO) Complete-data divergence No (underpenalizes)
Claeskens & Consentino Q-function divergence N/A (different target)
MI-AIC (this paper) Complete-data divergence Yes

Existing methods either (1) claim complete-data target but fail to fully correct (AICcd, PDIO), or (2) sidestep the problem by redefining the target (C&C). MI-AIC is the first to derive the correct penalty for the claimed goal of replicating complete-data model selection.

9. MI-BIC: Corrected BIC for Proper Multiple Imputation

The imputation bias correction applies to BIC as well, though the form differs slightly from MI-AIC.

Key Insight: Imputation Bias Affects the Deviance

The imputation bias arises from using $Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})$ to estimate $\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})$:

$$\mathbb{E}[Q(\hat{\theta}|\hat{\theta})] - \mathbb{E}[\ell_{com}(\hat{\theta})] = \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{mis|obs} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}) = \text{tr}(\text{RIV})$$

This bias needs to be corrected regardless of which information criterion we use. The correction is a property of the deviance estimation, not the penalty derivation.

AIC vs BIC: Different Penalty Derivations

AIC penalty comes from correcting optimism (expected overfit):

$$\text{Optimism} = \text{tr}(\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}) = Q + \text{tr}(\text{RIV})$$

So the AIC penalty includes an additional $\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$ term beyond $Q$.

BIC penalty comes from a Laplace approximation to the Bayes factor:

$$\text{BIC penalty} = Q \log(N)$$

This derivation does not involve $\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1}$, so there is no additional RIV term in the penalty.

Total Corrections

For MI-AIC:

  • Imputation bias correction: $2\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$
  • Optimism correction: $2Q + 2\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$
  • Total penalty: $2Q + 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$

For MI-BIC:

  • Imputation bias correction: $2\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$
  • BIC penalty: $Q \log(N)$
  • Total penalty: $Q \log(N) + 2\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$

MI-BIC Formula

MI-BIC (Corrected BIC for Proper Multiple Imputation): $$\boxed{\text{MI-BIC} = -2\bar{Q}_{MI}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}) + Q \log(N) + 2\text{tr}(\text{RIV})}$$

where:

  • $\bar{Q}_{MI}(\hat{\theta}_{obs}) = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^{M} \ell_{com}(\hat{\theta}_{obs} | Z^{obs}, \tilde{Z}^{mis,(m)})$
  • $Q$ = number of parameters
  • $N$ = sample size
  • $\text{RIV} = (1 + 1/M)W^{-1}B$ is the relative increase in variance matrix

Comparison of Penalties

Criterion Standard (complete data) MI-corrected (targeting complete-data)
AIC $2Q$ $2Q + 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$
BIC $Q \log(N)$ $Q \log(N) + 2\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$

Conditional Versions for Missing Covariates

Under distinct parameters, the conditional versions for regression model selection are:

$$\text{MI-AIC}_{Y|X} = -2Q_{Y|X}(\hat{\beta}|\hat{\theta}) + 2Q_\beta + 4\text{tr}(\text{RIV}_\beta)$$
$$\text{MI-BIC}_{Y|X} = -2Q_{Y|X}(\hat{\beta}|\hat{\theta}) + Q_\beta \log(N) + 2\text{tr}(\text{RIV}_\beta)$$
Why the factor of 2 vs 4?

MI-AIC has $4\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$ because AIC's optimism correction involves $\mathcal{I}_{com} \cdot \mathcal{I}_{obs}^{-1} = I_Q + \text{RIV}$, contributing an extra $\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$ beyond the base $Q$.

MI-BIC has only $2\text{tr}(\text{RIV})$ because BIC's penalty comes from Bayesian model comparison, which does not involve this ratio of information matrices.
